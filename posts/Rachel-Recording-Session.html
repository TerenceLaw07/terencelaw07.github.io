Rachel's Recording Sessions

14 July 2026

Day 1: Studio 2 – Drums, Spatial Tetris, and Clicks

Friday, May 15th



We kicked off the first day of recording in Studio 2, focusing entirely on rhythm. Our drummer, AJ, who I had recorded previously—brought in his own snare and cymbals.

Studio 2 is a fantastic but highly confined acoustic space. Setting up the kit immediately tested my mic stand management. To avoid low-frequency rumble traveling into the microphones, I had to play a careful game of spatial Tetris, ensuring none of my heavy mic stands physically touched or overlapped with AJ’s cymbal hardware.

Once the hardware was safe, I built out an extensive drum micing setup. Every choice was deliberate, balancing traditional workhorse techniques (Robjohns, 2003; Blomqvist, 2010; Gasson, n.d.) with a few bold experiments to capture both transient detail and raw, physical weight:

  • Kick Out: Neumann U87ai. Placed outside the resonant head, this was strictly to capture the low-end weight and physical "bigness" of the kick. It grabs the sub-harmonic bloom that dynamic mics miss, anchoring the kit towards a big sound (Brinck, no date; Pedersen and Grimshaw-Aagaard, 2018).

  • Kick In: Shure SM7B. Positioned halfway into the shell and slightly off-axis to the beater to grab the aggressive, focused attack.
  • Snare Top: Shure SM58 (Capsule Removed). Taking the grille off effectively turns this into an SM57. I placed the capsule right up against the head to capture the raw midrange body and initial stick crack, while rejecting the hi-hat.

  • Snare Bottom: Shure VP88. This was a massive success. The VP88 is a stereo M/S mic, but by routing just the Mono (Mid) side pointing straight up at the snare wires, it acted as a phase-coherent condenser, capturing the transient snap beautifully (Rumsey and McCormick, 2014).

  • Overheads: Neumann KM184. In a tight room like Studio 2, these small-diaphragm condensers gave me a fast, pristine stereo image of the cymbals without capturing problematic low-mid room reflections.

  • Toms: Shure SM58 on the Hi-Tom for punchy resonance, and a Shure Beta 57A on the Low Tom. The Beta 57A’s supercardioid pattern aggressively rejected the ride cymbal bleed while keeping the floor tom thick.

  • Hi-Hat: Coles 4038. Condensers can make hi-hats sound brittle. The ribbon element of the Coles naturally rolled off the piercing top-end, leaving a dark, smooth tone that won't fight Rachel's vocals later.

  • Wurst (Trash Mic): Neumann U87. I placed this premium condenser right in the center of the kit, above the kick and pointing at the drummer. It’s a bold move, but it captures a cohesive snapshot of the kick/snare relationship. Once heavily compressed in the DAW, this will be the ultimate "glue" for my drum bus.

  • Hall: SE4400. Deployed to capture the natural decay of the space.

After a quick soundcheck, Rachel and the producer/guitarist, Spencer, came in with a sorted demo of the first track. AJ is a pro—it took him two takes to figure out the groove, and we were recording. Rachel and Spencer popped in between takes with feedback, and we knocked the first song out incredibly smoothly. We then tracked the second, jazz-pop song called "Mr. Bean," which sounded fantastic and only required a few targeted punch-ins to match the vocal phrasing.



The 30-Minute Sprint: Finger Clicks With just half an hour left on the clock, we needed to track a group of four people doing finger clicks. I completely tore down the drum mics and set up a Blumlein closed-pair configuration using two Neumann U87s.



I placed the mics directly in the center of the four performers. Because they were all performing the exact same part, I routed a single, unified mix across four outputs on our Hearback system so everyone heard the exact same monitoring. The Blumlein technique kept everything perfectly phase-coherent (Huber, Runstein and Audio Engineering Society, 2018; AEA Ribbon Mics, 2018). The result? Incredibly snappy, crisp clicks that will sound like a massive crowd once I apply digital delay and reverb in the mix.

The Intermission: Session Housekeeping

Before moving into the second tracking day, I sat down and meticulously cleaned up the DAW sessions. When you are moving between studios and bringing in new musicians, having a messy template is a recipe for disaster. I color-coded, labeled, and routed everything so that Day 2 would be technically flawless.

Day 2: Studio 1 – Pianos, Brass, and The 3:30 PM Deadline

Monday, 18th May

Day 2 kicked off with an incredible amount of energy in Studio 1. Rachel was buzzing with excitement, her producer Spencer had invited a photographer friend to document the session, and AJ was hanging out in the room. The undisputed MVP of the day, however, was JJ—a brilliant multi-instrumentalist who tracked piano, trumpet, and violin.

The Piano & The Logic Tempo Pivot We started with JJ on the piano. To capture a wide, detailed image of the instrument, I utilized a multi-mic approach (Robjohns and White, 2004; Inside Blackbird, 2023):

  • A/B Spaced Pair: Two Neumann U87s capturing the left and right spread.
  • X/Y Overhead: A stereo X/Y pair positioned from above, looking directly down at the piano string (Rumsey and McCormick, 2014; Hejia, n.d.).

.

Almost immediately, we hit a production hurdle. Rachel and Spencer hadn't finalized a tempo change for the outro, meaning Rachel’s original guide vocals were sung at the song's standard, static tempo. Before we could record JJ's piano outro, I had to fix the foundation in Logic Pro. I cut the guide audio, went into the Global Track to program the new tempo curve, and utilized time-stretching on the audio regions to match the new grid. I kept looping playback for Rachel, making micro-adjustments until she was completely satisfied with the feel. Once the tempo mapped perfectly to her vision, JJ laid down the piano for the outro.



Trumpet & Taming the SPL Next, JJ pulled out his trumpet. Brass instruments are notoriously loud and move a massive amount of air, so close-miking requires caution (Robjohns, 1999). Furthermore, you rarely hear trumpets recorded entirely dry—they are designed to excite acoustic spaces. To achieve a larger-than-life brass sound, I set up a three-tier spatial capture:

  • Close/Bell: Neumann U87. Placed slightly above the bell rather than directly in front of it. This off-axis placement prevented the intense sound pressure levels (SPL) from overloading the capsule while maintaining high-end clarity.

  • Mid/Room: Rode M5s (ORTF). Set up in a closed-pair ORTF configuration directly in front of the trumpet to capture the immediate width and natural blooming of the brass in the room (Rumsey and McCormick, 2014; Brixen, n.d.; Sengpiel, 1997; RØDE Microphones, 2018).

  • Hall: Neumann U87. Placed far back in the hall to capture the pure acoustic response and decay of the space.


The Electric Violin The next overdub was JJ on violin. He brought an electric model, which completely changed the miking strategy. Because it didn't generate enough acoustic volume (dbSPL) to interact with the room, a hall mic was entirely unnecessary. Instead, I focused purely on capturing the physical articulation of the bow against the strings (Robjohns and White, 2004):

  • Close Mics: A Coles 4038 ribbon mic paired with a Neumann U87, both positioned incredibly close to where the bow strikes the strings.

Initially, the instrument's output jack was behaving badly, dropping signal. Fortunately, it stabilized, allowing me to capture a clean DI signal alongside the heavily textured close mics.

The 3:30 PM Deadline: Bass on the Clock With the violin wrapped, we looked at the clock. We were down to the wire, with just about 15 minutes left before we had to completely clear the room for a hard 3:30 PM studio cutoff. The pressure was on to capture Spencer's bass parts.

Despite the time crunch, I opted for a massive, three-channel hybrid capture. We combined the clean low-end of a DI signal with a heavily textured dual-mic setup on his Orange bass cabinet to ensure we had tonal flexibility in the mix:

  • Neumann U87: Placed coming down from the top, angled to face the edge of the amp speaker cone. This was utilized to capture the punchy midrange and articulation of the bass lines.

  • Coles 4038: Positioned on the edge of the amp from the right side. The ribbon element of the Coles is perfect for rounding out aggressive frequencies while capturing a massive, thick low-end response from the cabinet.

  • DI Signal: A direct line straight into the console to guarantee sub-frequency clarity and phase alignment during the digital mix phase.

Spencer strapped in and delivered a series of blisteringly fast, tight takes, nailing his parts right before the clock ran out.

Day 3: Vocals and Studio Psychology

Friday, 22nd May

The final day of tracking was dedicated entirely to Rachel's lead vocals. When capturing a commercial vocal, engineering goes far beyond microphone placement; it crosses into psychology and physical management (Crich, 2004).

For the vocal chain, I selected a Neumann U87. I mounted it upside down and positioned the capsule a little bit above her mouth, pointing at it at an off axis. This classic studio technique not only keeps the capsule clear of sheet music or lyric screens but effectively mitigates harsh plosives (pops) without solely relying on heavy filtering (Crich, 2004; Huber, Runstein and Audio Engineering Society, 2018).

Before we even rolled tape, while Rachel was at the mic making mock noises and clicks to test her levels, I noticed excessive mouth noise. I instantly knew she was dehydrated and called a pause to get her to drink more water. It is a small detail, but catching dehydration early saves hours of tedious de-clicking in post-production.

Rachel was incredibly professional and went through a solid warm-up routine before diving in. However, recording your own song for the first time carries a lot of weight. During playback of her initial takes, she seemed noticeably displeased with the results. As an engineer, my job is to listen objectively: she was perfectly in time and perfectly in tune. The technical execution was flawless.

I realized she wasn't missing the notes; she was overthinking the performance. I stepped in and suggested that what she was actually missing was the "Curiosity of Mr. Bean" (referencing the specific vibe of the track). Sometimes, you have to remind a singer of their own original vision to help them get out of their head. As we continued running repeats, you could hear her start to find her sound. It was a meticulous process—she did multiple takes of several sections to ensure we captured the perfect emotional arc.

The Reality of Studio Time Because we took the necessary time to let her explore and find that perfect lead vocal, the only drawback was running out of time for ad-libs. We captured a few, but Rachel understandably wanted more layers to thicken the arrangement.

With her leaving the university for the summer, our in-person studio schedule had effectively maxed out. To finish the production, we transitioned into a remote workflow. Rachel recorded the remaining ad-libs at her home setup, and the producer, Spencer, tracked the final guitar layers at his place, sending the stems over to me remotely.
